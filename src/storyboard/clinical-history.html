Development History: From P-188 to Vepoloxamer

There have been two distinct Phase III sickle cell trials across this molecule's history — conducted 15 years apart, by different sponsors, with different compounds, different designs, and different lessons. Understanding the difference between them is essential context for any serious investor.

1980s — Sponsor: CytRx Corporation / Burroughs Wellcome

P-188 (Non-Purified) — Orphan Drug Designation & Phase 2 Sickle Cell Pilot

Dr. Robert Hunter at Emory University identifies poloxamer 188's rheologic, cytoprotective, and antithrombotic properties. His work leads to formation of CytRx Corporation, which licenses the inventions from Emory and develops the drug under the name RheothRx. CytRx secures FDA Orphan Drug Designation for sickle cell disease (1989) and forms a major research alliance with Burroughs Wellcome (later acquired by Glaxo, now GSK).

Burroughs Wellcome conducts a Phase 2 pilot study in sickle cell disease (n=50) using non-purified P-188 (RheothRx), which shows markedly reduced VOC duration, reduced pain intensity, and reduced analgesic use in patients receiving the full dose. Encouraged by these results, they initiate a large acute myocardial infarction (AMI) trial program with up to 20,000 patients planned.

⚠ Renal toxicity signal in the large AMI lead-in study (~3,000 patients) halts the program. CytRx investigates and determines the cause: low-molecular-weight impurities in commercial-grade P-188 are preferentially absorbed by kidney proximal tubule cells, causing reversible osmotic nephrosis. Glaxo returns the program to CytRx.
Early 1990s — Sponsor: CytRx Corporation

The Purification Breakthrough — Birth of Vepoloxamer

CytRx develops a proprietary manufacturing method using supercritical fluid chromatography to remove the low-molecular-weight impurities from commercial P-188. The resulting purified compound eliminates the renal accumulation problem while maintaining full therapeutic activity at higher doses. This purified entity is later assigned the United States Adopted Name (USAN) "vepoloxamer" — and it is this purified compound, and its novel manufacturing method, that the Composition of Matter patent (US 9,403,941; expiry 2035) protects.

✓ Purification resolves the AMI-era safety problem. The molecule that will go forward into all subsequent trials is now fundamentally different from commercial poloxamer 188.
PHASE III TRIAL #1 — 1998–1999 — Sponsor: CytRx Corporation

CytRx Phase III Sickle Cell Trial (Purified P-188 / Pre-Vepoloxamer INN)

Trial Design
  • Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled
  • Conducted at 40 US medical centers
  • Enrollment: March 1998 – October 1999
  • Patients: n=255 (planned n=350 — concluded early)
  • Ages: 9–53 years
  • Drug: purified poloxamer 188, 100 mg/kg load (1h) then 30 mg/kg/h (47h)
  • PI: E.P. Orringer, MD (Univ. of North Carolina)
Primary Endpoint & Result
  • Endpoint: duration of painful episode from randomization to crisis resolution
  • Primary endpoint: not met — 9-hour reduction, not statistically significant at n=255
  • Trial ended early — capital constraints, not safety
  • Post-hoc responder analysis (n=249 ITT): 50% vepoloxamer vs. 37% placebo achieved crisis resolution within 168h (p=0.02)
  • Subgroup signals: children (−21h) and hydroxyurea co-treatment (−16h) showed stronger effects
Why it missed: The trial was underpowered — enrollment stopped at 255 of the 350 planned patients due to capital constraints, not safety. The primary endpoint was designed to detect a treatment difference across the full population; the post-hoc analysis showed that the trial lacked statistical power to detect the signal that was there. Endpoint design limitations (use of a 168h default cap for non-resolvers) also compressed the apparent effect. Both issues were identified and directly incorporated into the design of the next Phase III.

Corporate fate: Following the trial, CytRx merged with a private company and discontinued all existing programs including vepoloxamer to focus on the merged entity's assets. In 2004, a group including Dr. Hunter formed SynthRx, Inc. and acquired all rights to the compound's data, know-how, and IP from CytRx.

Publication: Orringer EP et al. JAMA. 2001;286(17):2099–2106. PMID: 11694150.
2004–2010 — SynthRx, Inc. / Adventrx Pharmaceuticals

Asset Consolidation & IP Strengthening

SynthRx acquires all rights from CytRx (2004). In 2010, Adventrx Pharmaceuticals acquires SynthRx, renames the compound ANX-188, and begins pre-clinical and FDA interaction work. Adventrx subsequently renames itself Mast Therapeutics.

New intellectual property developed and filed. Composition of Matter patent application advances. No clinical activity during this period.
PHASE III TRIAL #2 — 2013–2016 — Sponsor: Mast Therapeutics (NYSE: MSTX)

EPIC Trial — Evaluation of Purified Poloxamer 188 In Vaso-Occlusive Crisis

How it differed from Trial #1: Mast specifically designed EPIC to address the two known weaknesses of the CytRx trial — statistical underpowering and endpoint ambiguity. GMP manufacturing was reestablished from scratch with partner Pierre Fabre (France) using the proprietary supercritical fluid chromatography purification process. FDA provided written agreement on the trial design prior to initiation.

Trial Design
  • Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled
  • 75 sites across 14 countries
  • Enrollment: Jan 2013 – Feb 2016
  • Patients: n=388 (1.5× the CytRx trial)
  • Ages: 4–46 years (predominantly pediatric)
  • Drug: vepoloxamer (MST-188), same dosing regimen
  • PI: James F. Casella, MD (Johns Hopkins)
  • New primary endpoint: time to last parenteral opioid (more objective than crisis resolution)
Primary Endpoint & Result
  • Endpoint: duration of VOC (time to last parenteral opioid)
  • Primary endpoint: not met — 82h vepoloxamer vs. 78h placebo (p=0.09)
  • Vepoloxamer arm was numerically longer — unexpected and poorly understood
  • Safety: clean — no deaths, no significant AE differential vs. placebo
  • Secondary endpoints: no significant differences in re-hospitalization or acute chest syndrome
Why it missed — and what it means: Unlike the CytRx trial, EPIC was adequately powered. The primary endpoint was not simply underpowered — the effect was genuinely not detected in this design and this endpoint, in this patient population (younger, heavier hydroxyurea use than the CytRx cohort). The 82h vs. 78h result (vepoloxamer numerically longer) remains unexplained and is the subject of ongoing academic discussion. Possible explanations include a longer and more thorough treatment duration enabling more complete opioid taper (a meaningful clinical outcome reframed as a longer "crisis"), confounding from hydroxyurea co-therapy in a predominantly pediatric population, and endpoint sensitivity issues. Mast terminated all clinical development programs in September 2016 and merged with Savara Inc. in April 2017.

What EPIC generated: The world's largest placebo-controlled vaso-occlusive crisis dataset. One complete filed IND (the EPIC IND) and a draft IMPD (Heart Failure; drafted but not submitted). A comprehensive human safety and PK characterization. All of this transferred to LifeRaft as part of the asset acquisition from Savara.

Publication: Casella JF et al. JAMA. 2021;325(15):1513–1523. PMID: 33877274. doi:10.1001/jama.2021.3414. (Published 2021 despite 2016 data — reflecting the scientific value of the dataset for the SCD research community. This is the definitive published reference for the EPIC trial results.)
2020 — Vepoloxamer Heart Failure Study · Published Data

Vepoloxamer in Heart Failure — Rapid, Sustained Improvement in Cardiac Function

In a canine microembolization heart failure model (baseline LVEF ~30%), vepoloxamer at 450 mg/kg improved left ventricular ejection fraction by 11 absolute percentage points within 2 hours, with benefits persisting for one week after a single infusion. A "pulsed therapy" paradigm — two infusions three weeks apart — sustained improvement through six weeks. This study demonstrates that vepoloxamer can produce clinically meaningful functional improvement in a failing heart — a distinct therapeutic proposition from the acute rescue mechanism studied in STEMI and the VOC mechanism studied in SCD.

Note: This study was conducted under a collaboration involving LifeRaft-affiliated investigators. Mast Therapeutics initiated a Phase 2 trial of vepoloxamer in chronic heart failure (NCT02596477; randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled in ambulatory CHF patients with LVEF ≤35%) that enrolled 10 patients before termination in October 2016 for business/strategic reasons — not for safety concerns.

✓ Published vepoloxamer-specific data showing acute and sustained cardiac functional improvement. Pulsed dosing paradigm suggests viability for chronic disease management. Heart failure IMPD (draft, not submitted) included in asset transfer.

Sabbah HN et al. Acute and sustained hemodynamic effects of poloxamer 188 in an experimental model of heart failure. Cardiovasc Drugs Ther. 2020;34(6):783–792. PMID: 32146638.

2017 — Asset Acquisition · LifeRaft Biosciences

Full Asset Acquisition — LifeRaft Acquires Vepoloxamer from Savara

Following the EPIC program termination in September 2016, Mast Therapeutics merged with Savara Inc. in April 2017, at which point the Vepoloxamer asset transferred to Savara. LifeRaft Biosciences subsequently acquired Vepoloxamer from Savara under the terms of an Asset Purchase Agreement (October 2017). The asset transfer includes: the Composition of Matter patent (US 9,403,941, expiry 2035), one complete filed IND (EPIC trial IND) and a draft IMPD (Heart Failure; drafted but not submitted), clinical records and data from prior programs, manufacturing process documentation and formulation know-how, and the published scientific literature base.

2017 — Present · LifeRaft Biosciences & MUSC

Value Creation: New Indication Development & Method of Use Patent Application

Since acquiring the asset, LifeRaft has actively advanced Vepoloxamer into a new and commercially differentiated indication — radioprotection — in collaboration with the Medical University of South Carolina (MUSC). This work represents genuine post-acquisition value creation: a new clinical hypothesis, new preclinical studies, and a new intellectual property layer added to the asset.

Preclinical studies conducted through the LifeRaft / MUSC collaboration have demonstrated Vepoloxamer's activity as a systemic radioprotectant — protecting non-insulted (healthy) tissue from collateral radiation damage. The results of these studies are summarized in the Preclinical Evidence section of this site.

IP Created Post-Acquisition — Method of Use Patent Application

Based on the MUSC collaboration studies, MUSC has filed a Method of Use patent application covering the administration of Vepoloxamer (P188) as a radioprotectant — specifically, protecting non-insulted cells from radiation damage via pre-exposure administration (10–150 mg/kg, within a 0–48 hour window before exposure). The application's claims reflect a precise dosing and timing protocol developed through the experimental program.

LifeRaft holds an exclusive license to this patent application (and any patents issuing therefrom) under a license agreement with MUSC, including the right to sublicense. The license covers both the Patent Rights (exclusive, with sublicense rights) and associated Know-How (non-exclusive, with sublicense rights) within the field of use.

EPIC Result Interpretation

A failed primary endpoint is not the same as a failed molecule. The EPIC trial generated the world's most comprehensive dataset on vaso-occlusive crisis and surfaced important insights into endpoint design that a next-generation trial could address. The safety story is the most commercially valuable output.

What EPIC Established
  • Clean safety profile in 388 patients ages 4–46, 12 countries
  • No patient deaths; no significant AE differential vs. placebo
  • Largest placebo-controlled SCD trial ever completed
  • One filed IND (EPIC) plus a draft IMPD (Heart Failure) the next owner can build upon immediately
  • Comprehensive pharmacokinetic and clinical characterization
What EPIC Left Open
  • Whether a redesigned endpoint would reveal signal in SCD
  • Whether biomarker-selected patients would respond more strongly
  • Efficacy in STEMI, ischemic stroke, TBI, oral mucositis — never clinically tested
  • Radioprotection — no clinical trial ever conducted in this indication
