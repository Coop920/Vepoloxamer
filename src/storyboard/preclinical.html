STEMI / Reperfusion Injury

Standard-of-care treatment (PCI/thrombolytics) itself causes the second, often larger injury — reperfusion-induced oxidative damage to viable cardiac tissue. There is no approved pharmacological intervention for this. Vepoloxamer's core mechanism maps directly onto this window: administered intracoronary at reperfusion, it seals cardiomyocyte membranes before the oxidative burst propagates.

Market: Approximately 805,000 Americans experience a myocardial infarction annually (US TAM). Cardioprotection at reperfusion is a well-recognized but unmet clinical need across interventional cardiology. Source: American Heart Association. Heart Disease and Stroke Statistics — 2024 Update. Circulation. 2024;149:e347–e913.

Evidence: Porcine STEMI model — intracoronary P188 at reperfusion significantly improved mitochondrial yield and infarct size vs. control. The delivery timing (intracoronary, at the catheterization lab) is already part of standard PCI workflow, easing clinical implementation. Separately, a 2025 study published in Scientific Reports (Nature) demonstrated that P188's cardioprotective effects are mediated through increased endothelial nitric oxide production — an additional mechanism beyond membrane sealing. Isolated adult cardiomyocyte studies confirmed dose-dependent protection at reoxygenation, with the hydrophobic PPO block essential for activity.

Ischemic Stroke

tPA is the only approved acute therapy for ischemic stroke — but it carries significant hemorrhagic risk and a narrow 4.5-hour therapeutic window. Preclinical data suggest Vepoloxamer, as an adjunct to tPA, can accelerate thrombolysis, reduce infarct volume, and attenuate tPA's own microvascular side effects. This is a combination strategy that could extend tPA's utility, not replace it — a lower regulatory and commercial hurdle.

Market: ~795,000 strokes annually in the US, of which approximately 87% are ischemic (~690,000/year) (US TAM). Stroke is the #5 cause of US death and a leading cause of disability. Source: CDC Stroke Facts, citing Tsao CW et al. Heart Disease and Stroke Statistics — 2023 Update. Circulation. 2023;147:e93–e621.

Evidence: Vepoloxamer monotherapy reduced infarct volume by 25% in rat embolic stroke; combination tPA + Vepoloxamer reduced infarct volume by 32% and significantly improved neurological outcomes vs. either agent alone. Findings replicated in aged male and female animals — the population most representative of real-world stroke patients. A long-term study demonstrated that P188 alleviated brain atrophy, improved motor function, and increased survival at 3 weeks post-stroke, with MMP-9 inhibition and BBB preservation confirmed as mechanisms.

→ Wang et al. (2019): Vepoloxamer enhances fibrinolysis of tPA on acute ischemic stroke — Stroke (AHA) / PMC6878196 → Zhang et al. (2023): Vepoloxamer + tPA reduces neurovascular damage in aged male and female rats — Front Neurol / PMID 37869138 → Gu et al. (2013): P188 reduces infarct volume, brain atrophy, and improves long-term survival — PLoS ONE / PMID 23613890
tPA Combination Strategy · No Approved Neuroprotective Agent

Traumatic Brain Injury

Market: Approximately 224,000 TBI-related hospitalizations and an estimated 1.5–2.5 million total TBI events annually in the US (all severity levels) (US TAM). No FDA-approved neuroprotective drug for acute TBI. Secondary microthrombosis and neuroinflammation are the primary drivers of long-term disability. Sources: CDC Traumatic Brain Injury Data; Taylor CA et al. MMWR Surveill Summ. 2017;66(SS-9):1–16.

Window: Acute rescue — within hours post-injury, Vepoloxamer attenuates blood-brain barrier disruption, microthrombosis, and astrocyte activation.

Evidence: Published preclinical studies demonstrate P188 administered 2h post-TBI reduced lesion size, tissue loss, and GFAP+ astrocyte activation, with improved sensorimotor and cognitive recovery in rat models. A 2024 dose-response and therapeutic window study from Henry Ford Health established that vepoloxamer at 300 mg/kg IV was optimal (more effective than 600 mg/kg), with a therapeutic window extending to at least 3 days post-TBI — critical pharmacology data for clinical trial design. In 2025, Alatrash et al. reported for the first time that P188 crosses the blood-brain barrier following IV administration in mice post-TBI and is actively transported into damaged brain endothelial cells via endocytosis.

→ Zhang et al. (2018): Treatment of TBI with Vepoloxamer — J Neurotrauma / PMC5900497 → Chen et al. (2024): Vepoloxamer dose-response and therapeutic window in TBI — Neurochemistry Int / PMC10872547 → Alatrash et al. (2025): P188 active transport in brain endothelial cells; BBB crossing demonstrated in vivo — J Biomed Sci Eng / PMC12419450 → Systematic Review (2024): Effects of P188 on TBI — PubMed 38590762
High Unmet Need · No Approved Drug · BBB Penetration Demonstrated · Optimal Dose Identified
☢️

Radiation-Induced Oral Mucositis

Market: No FDA-approved drug specifically for radiation-induced oral mucositis in head and neck cancer radiotherapy. ~72,000 new head and neck cancer diagnoses annually in the US (NCI 2025 estimate), the majority of whom receive radiotherapy. Up to 90% of patients treated with concurrent chemoradiation develop oral mucositis; severe Grade 3–4 mucositis occurs in 30–66% of patients (US TAM). Sources: AACR Head and Neck Cancers Overview (NCI 2025 estimate); Trotti A et al. Radiother Oncol. 2003;66:253–262.

Window: Prophylactic — Vepoloxamer on board prior to radiation shields mucosal cells from the oxidative cascade of ionizing radiation.

Clinical Opportunity: Oral mucositis offers a clean, well-defined primary endpoint (WHO mucositis grading scale), a defined patient population receiving a scheduled insult (radiation), and a straightforward PoC trial design — making it one of the most capital-efficient paths to clinical proof of concept for the Vepoloxamer platform. The prophylactic mechanism is the same as in the radioprotection indication, applied locally to the most affected tissue.

→ Lee et al. (2004): P188 prevents acute necrosis of irradiated skeletal muscle cells — PubMed 15302418

Platform Breadth — Published Evidence Across Additional Indications

The lead indications above represent the deepest evidence and clearest development paths. The following indications have published preclinical support demonstrating Vepoloxamer's activity across a broader range of pathologies — all driven by the same core mechanism of action. Each links to a representative publication.

Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)

P188 corrected calcium overload in mdx cardiomyocytes and prevented acute cardiac failure during dobutamine stress in vivo, published in Nature. Subcutaneous delivery has been shown to confer significant skeletal muscle protection. Orphan drug pathway available.

→ Yasuda et al. (2005) — Nature. PMID: 16025101 → Houang et al. (2015) — Mol Ther Methods. PMID: 26623440

Hemorrhagic Shock

P188 added to resuscitation fluid increased survival from 0% to 75% in 50% volume-controlled hemorrhage (p<0.001), prolonged survivable duration of hypotensive resuscitation by >3 hours, and prevented multi-organ injury. Independently validated by the US Army Institute of Surgical Research.

→ Zhang et al. (2009) — Shock. PMID: 19197229 → Burns et al. (2011) — Resuscitation. PMID: 21733612

Burn Injury

P188 prevented zone-of-stasis formation within 2 hours post-burn, attenuated wound deepening in deep second-degree burns, and showed benefit across 33 preclinical studies in a 2024 PRISMA-compliant systematic review.

→ Mutore et al. (2024) — Shock. PMID: 39178216

Radioprotection (Systemic)

P188 prevents acute necrosis of irradiated skeletal muscle cells via membrane stabilization. No FDA-approved pre-exposure systemic radioprotector exists. BARDA and NIAID maintain active funding pipelines for agents in this class.

→ Lee et al. (2004) — Surgery. PMID: 15302418

Peripheral Limb Ischemia

In a murine hind-limb ischemia model, P188 reduced myocyte injury by 84% vs. controls (p<0.001) and improved 7-day survival (75% vs. 25%). Same reperfusion injury mechanism as STEMI, applied to surgical revascularization.

→ Murphy et al. (2010) — Plast Reconstr Surg. PMID: 20517088

Spinal Cord Injury

A single subcutaneous P188 injection 6h post-SCI preserved cord integrity, restored nerve conduction through the lesion, and produced behavioral recovery. Extended to naturally occurring canine paraplegia with unexpectedly rapid recovery.

→ Borgens et al. (2004) — J Neurosci Res. PMID: 15048938

A Rare Non-Dilutive Funding Pathway — Multiple Indications, Multiple Agencies

Most preclinical drug assets offer a single development track: raise private capital, assume full clinical risk. Vepoloxamer is different. Three of its evidence-supported indications — systemic radioprotection, burn injury, and hemorrhagic shock — qualify for government and defense funding mechanisms that could underwrite meaningful early-stage development at zero equity cost to a strategic acquirer.

Radioprotection: BARDA & NIAID
BARDA's Radiological and Nuclear Medical Countermeasures Program and NIAID's Radiation and Nuclear Countermeasures Program both maintain active funding pipelines for radioprotective agents. No FDA-approved pre-exposure radioprotector exists — Vepoloxamer's published preclinical data and its established mechanism of action place it directly in this programmatic scope.

Burn Injury: DoD / USAMRDC & BARDA
Burn injuries are among the most prevalent combat trauma presentations. The US Army Medical Research and Development Command (USAMRDC) and the Department of Defense maintain active funding programs for novel burn wound therapeutics. A 2024 systematic review from UT Southwestern explicitly calls for clinical trials of P188 in burns.

Hemorrhagic Shock: DoD / USAISR
The US Army Institute of Surgical Research has independently funded and validated P188 as a small-volume resuscitation additive, with published survival data in a miniature swine hemorrhage model. Field-deployable, low-volume resuscitation solutions are an active DoD procurement priority.

Taken together, these pathways represent a capital-efficient, high-upside development structure essentially unavailable to most pre-clinical assets. A strategic acquirer with government contracting infrastructure could pursue non-dilutive funding in parallel with commercial indication development. No government funding commitment exists at this time; this is a potential pathway that a qualified acquirer would be well-positioned to evaluate and pursue.
